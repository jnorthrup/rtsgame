## Introduction

These definitions will shape how players interact with resources, technologies, and territory. ## A. Defining "Access"

"Access" refers to the ability of a player or faction to utilize or benefit from specific game elements. Lack of access might mean an element is unknown, unavailable, or unusable.

**Key Areas of Access:**

1.  **Access to Raw Materials (Regolith, Elemental Veins):**
    *   *Description:* The ability to locate and be in a position to potentially harvest basic regolith or specialized elemental veins. This might be tied to sensor technology, exploration, or proximity.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Establishing a *Resource Appraisal Concession* to survey and identify viable *Extraction Frontiers*."

2.  **Access to Conversion Technologies (Matter-to-Energy/Resources):**
    *   *Description:* The ability to use or develop specific processes for converting raw materials into energy (Wattage types) or other processed resources. This could involve research, reverse-engineering, or acquiring specific hardware.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Securing *Transmutation Blueprints* to enable *Flux Converters* for advanced energy generation."

3.  **Access to Generative AI (Component Design, Process Optimization, Strategic Advice):**
    *   *Description:* The ability to utilize the game's Generative AI for specific tasks. This might be tiered, with basic AI functions more readily available than advanced strategic or design capabilities.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Unlocking a *Cognitive Core Interface* to gain access to the *Noospheric Design Network* for component generation."

4.  **Access to Strategic Terrain (Chokepoints, High Ground, Shielded Areas):**
    *   *Description:* The ability to reach, perceive, and potentially utilize terrain features that offer tactical or strategic advantages.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Deploying *Pioneer Beacons* to chart paths to *Occlusion Zones* or *Dominance Apexes*."

## B. Defining "Control"

"Control" implies a deeper level of command and authority over game elements than mere "Access." Control allows a player or faction to dictate the operation, output, or status of these elements, often to the exclusion of others.

**Key Areas of Control:**

1.  **Control over Resource Extraction/Conversion (Rate, Type, Efficiency):**
    *   *Description:* The ability to manage and direct how resources are extracted and processed. This includes setting extraction rates, choosing output types (e.g., specific Wattage or refined materials), and optimizing efficiency.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Implementing *Governor Protocols* on *Regolith Harvesters* to dictate *Yield Quotas* and *Energy Conversion Ratios*."

2.  **Control over Manufacturing (What is built, Queue, Resources used):**
    *   *Description:* The ability to dictate what units or structures are produced, manage production queues, and allocate resources to manufacturing processes.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Exercising *Fabricator Primacy* to schedule *Mass-Driver Assembly Lines* and prioritize *Alloy Commitment*."

3.  **Control over Generative AI (Direction, Parameters, Learning Data):**
    *   *Description:* Beyond just using AI, this is the ability to guide its development, set its operational parameters, or even influence its learning datasets to tailor its outputs or advice.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Asserting *Sentience Mandates* over the *Strategic Oracle Engine* to refine its *Threat Axioms* and *Victory Heuristics*."

4.  **Control over Territory & Consumability (Denial, Fortification, Resource Depletion):**
    *   *Description:* The ability to exert dominance over a region, including denying access to enemies, fortifying locations, and managing the rate at which local resources are consumed or depleted.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Establishing an *Exclusion Perimeter* with *Bastion Emplacements*, managing the *Territorial Resource Drawdown*."

5.  **Control over Energy Grids (Distribution, Prioritization, Overload/Brownout):**
    *   *Description:* The ability to manage the flow of power across a network, prioritize energy allocation to critical systems, and potentially risk overloading parts of the grid for boosts or suffering brownouts if mismanaged.
    *   *Thematic Terminology Example:* "Regulating *Wattage Conduits* via the *Grid Command Nexus*, shunting power to *Shield Emitters* while risking *Substation Burnout*."
